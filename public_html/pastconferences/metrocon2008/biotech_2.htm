New Approaches for the Treatment of Glaucoma
Thomas Yorio
University of North Texas Health Science Center

Glaucoma is one of the leading causes of blindness in the world today. This disease appears to have many potential causes, including, for some individuals, a genetic predisposition. However, this latter group only comprises less than 10% of all glaucoma patients, so a number of other mechanisms appear to be important for the development of this disease. Traditionally, glaucoma has been treated by reducing the intra ocular pressure using a variety of pharmacological approaches. This includes reducing the formation of aqueous humor or effecting processes responsible for aqueous humor outflow.  As we begin to learn more about the normal physiology of aqueous humor formation and outflow, additional targets have been identified. For example, the outflow pathway itself, the pathological site in primary open angle glaucoma (POAG), has been seen as a target for new drug, device and surgical treatments. All of these modalities are centered on increasing fluid removal from the eye. One of the unique features of the eye is that it is easily accessible for local drug delivery. However, there are still a number of barriers present that require drugs to have certain physiochemical characteristics for maximum drug penetration in order to act on a particular mechanism, such as aqueous humor formation. Therefore, new approaches use a variety of techniques to enhance drug delivery and to get more selective actions.  These include: pro-drug formulations, use of biodegradable penetration enhancers, bio-adhesives and biodegradable polymers for long term delivery or more recently, coupling drug to nanoparticles or targeting specific genes using gene therapy. Unfortunately, these latter techniques are only recently been introduced and additional studies are needed before their efficacy is determined.

Dr. Yorio received his Ph.D. in Pharmacology from Mount Sinai School of Medicine in New York in 1975.  He remained at Sinai for a brief Postdoctoral appointment where he was a National Science Foundation Fellow and a National Kidney Foundation Fellow.  In 1977 he joined the UNT Health Science Center as an Assistant Professor and rose in rank to Professor in Pharmacology and Neuroscience.  He was selected as the Founding Dean of the Graduate School of Biomedical Sciences in 1993 and this year as Vice President for Research while continuing to serve as Dean. Under his leadership the graduate school has tripled its research funding over the past six years. Dr. Yorio has himself maintained an actively funded research program supported by the National Institutes of Health (NIH) and pharmaceutical industry and is recognized nationally and internationally for his work in glaucoma and signal transduction.

Dr. Yorio is an active collaborator with Alcon Research Ltd., and has been involved in numerous projects involving translational research with Alcon and other pharmaceutical industries. He has been the recipient of the Texas Higher Education Coordinating Board’s Advanced Research and Advanced Technology grants that resulted in the development of potentially new glaucoma therapeutics.
