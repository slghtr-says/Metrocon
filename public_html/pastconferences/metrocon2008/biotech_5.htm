Micro- and nano-patterned features on implant surfaces for reduction of biofilm formation
Karen S. Pawlowski, Debra Wawro, Robert Magnusson, TH Chrzanowski, R. W. Chan, and P. S. Roland
UT Southwestern Medical Center, Resonant Sensors, Inc., University of Texas at Arlington

Implantable medical device technology has made such great advances recently that medical implant surgeries are considered routine, with young and old patients receiving implants that are expected to remain in their bodies indefinitely.  Unfortunately, these implants have the potential for device failure due to growth of micro-organisms on their surface in the form of biofilms. The longer the device remains in the body, the higher the likelihood of biofilm formation and patient infection.   Biofilm formation on medical implants is common enough that it is now characterized as an infectious disease, chronic polymer-associated infection (CPAI, Donlan 2001, Hall-Stodley et al. 2005). To date, essentially all materials used for surgical implantation have the potential to grow biofilms. Currently, twenty-eight classes of human infections have been associated with biofilms (Costerton et al. 1999). The increased expectancies of mechanical life for many of these devices have made CPAI a growing problem.  Often, the only remedy for the recurrent infections caused by the biofilms is removal of an infected implant, which is physically and emotionally traumatic and expensive.

A subtle and inexpensive method to reduce biofilm formation on implants without instigating an untoward response from the body (or host) is the focus of this research.  The method presented involves altering the surface properties of the implant by topological patterning with micro- and nano- structured features to reduce or eliminate the ability of bacteria to form large, confluent biofilms.  Topological surface modifications present the possibility of improving material longevity without drastic changes to the existing devices or without prolonged exposure to antibiotics which increases the potential for the development of antibiotic resistant strains of bacteria. 

This interdisciplinary work integrates three major efforts: the basic biology of biofilm formation by microbes, the interaction of the host with an implanted “foreign body” and current advances in micro- and nano-fabrication engineering.  While this work addresses chronic polymer-associated infection, biofilms are not just a medical problem. They form on, essentially, any wetted surface and cause problems for industries ranging from microchip manufacture to water handling systems, to timed drug delivery and space travel.  This research has broad applicability and the potential to promote further research in the role of surface architecture in reducing biofilm formation and altering wound healing.

Dr. Pawlowski has worked for more than 20 years in biology of the peripheral auditory system.  In recent years, she and her colleagues are among a small number of researchers that have documented the formation of biofilms on explanted components of cochlear implants.  She is working with major cochlear implant manufacturers to determine which characteristics of the implant contribute to biofilm formation. In parallel, she and her colleagues are developing implant material with specific surface pattern features in order to determine if a subtle, inexpensive change to the implant can reduce biofilm formation without causing abnormal wound healing.
