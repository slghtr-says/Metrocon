Quantum Cryptology
Tom Frey, Lockheed Martin
8:30-9:30

Abstract: Quantum Cryptology is an emerging field that offers a very secure method to transfer information. This technology leap is based on the novel properties of quantum mechanics to ensure theoretically perfect encryption as well as protection against eavesdropping. This presentation discusses the fundament properties behind quantum cryptology, the advantages of this
Quantum Cryptology over traditional methods, potential applications, and some of the obstacles still ahead.

Biography: Dr. Tom Frey is a Principle Research Scientist for Lockheed Martin where he leads a team of engineers in the creation and development of new technologies for current and futures aircraft. Previously, he served as the Chief Engineer of the F-22 Raptor Communications, Navigation, and Identification System and a technical consultant to the F-35 program. Dr. Frey has published more than a dozen technical papers and holds patents in the fields of high gain antennas and statistical signal processing. He is an instructor for the Engineering Leadership Development Program on Digital Signal Processing and has served as an adjunct professor in the Department of Engineering at Texas Christian University and the Department of Computer & Mathematics Sciences at Columbia College. Dr. Frey is member of the Texas A&M External Advisory & Development Council for the Department of Electrical Engineering. He is a Senior Member of the IEEE and a registered Professional Engineer in the state of Texas.

Smart Antennas
Scott Bergen, Focus Antennas

Abstract: Adaptive, smart, and semi-smart antenna technologies are now available to to improve capacity and throughput on wireless networks. Interferometric Sensing of MEMS and Biological Structures
Tristan Tayag, Texas Christian University
1:30-3:00

Abstract: Optical interferometry is an attractive measurement technique due to its high resolution, large dynamic range, absolute calibration, and non-contacting detection. Modern interferometers utilize fiber optics to form robust systems for applications, which range from sensing the motion of translation stages in atomic force microscopes to sensing the distributed strain within the wing of an aircraft. In this paper, we review key aspects in the implementation of an interferometer system and present experimental results on two specific areas of application. The optical architecture and demodulation scheme are key aspects of an interferometer that are inherently interrelated. A common objective in interferometer design is the robustness of the system to environmental perturbations unrelated to the signal of interest. This objective, along with the form of the measurand, often dictates the optical configuration and demodulation approach. A variety of two-beam interferometer architectures and demodulation techniques will be reviewed. Particular emphasis will be placed on demodulation schemes based on digital signal processing (DSP) techniques. DSP-based demodulation schemes offer computational sophistication, real-time operation, and system flexibility. The two application areas, which will be presented, involve sensing the motion of microelectromechanical system (MEMS) structures and the contraction of biological cell structures. Each of these application areas has distinct system requirements that drive the interferometer design. The background and motivation for these two applications will be presented along with experimental results.

Biography: Tristan J. Tayag received his BSEE and MSE degrees from the Johns Hopkins University in 1986 and 1987, respectively, and his PhD degree from the University of Virginia in 1991. In 1986, he joined the Applied Physics Laboratory of the Johns Hopkins University where he conducted research in fiber optic sensors for biomedical applications. From 1991 through 1997, he was engaged in the development of integrated optical signal processors at the U.S. Army Research Laboratory. He served as a Visiting Scientist at the U.S. Military Academy at West Point during the 1996 academic year. Currently, he is an Associate Professor at Texas Christian University where his research interests include optical sensing, microelectromechanical systems, and digital signal processing. He holds several patents and has published over 60 technical papers on these subjects. He is a member of the Institute of Electrical and Electronic Engineers, the Optical Society of America, and SPIE.

MuSUEC (Multiple Sensor Ultrasonic Enabled Communication
Jim Houston, Lockheed Martin
3:15-4:45

Abstract: The introduction of advanced support concepts in military systems like Prognostic Health Management (PHM) and Autonomic Logistics has brought with it the need for a significant number of additional sensors. Sensors in these sorts of applications are typically associated with non-mission critical, low bandwidth data streams (environmental data, PHM precursors, etc). Collection and distribution of these data streams with "wired" solutions is impractical because of the associated costs, difficulty of installation, reliability and weight impacts. While a wireless solution is clearly indicated, RF energy-based solutions like BluetoothTM are often blocked by structural elements in the aircraft and have a negative impact on aircraft electromagnetic compatibility and signature. This presentation addresses a new wireless technology based on digitally modulated ultrasonic energy that appears to offer significant promise for the solution of this sensor integration problem. Challenges in the utilization of this technology on an aircraft and potential technology solutions to these challenges will be addressed. The presentation will end with a discussion of potential synergistic commercial applications for this technology and future plans for development of the technology in airborne weapon systems.

Biography: Jim Houston is a Research and Development Staff Specialist for Lockheed Martin Aeronautics Company. He is the Principle Investigator for a R&D project which is examining risk factors associated with the utilization of COTS based avionics in military systems as well as potential technologies which could mitigate these risks. He has been with GD/Lockheed since 1976. He graduated with a MSEE from Oklahoma State University.
