Convergent Technologies for Innovative Biotechnology Product Development: From Concept to Bio- Manufacturing
Mary Pat Moyer
INCELL Corporation, LLC

Convergence of biotechnology applications and engineering fields is leading to innovations in product development and manufacturing.  New vaccines and regenerative medicine products in the INCELL pipeline are bringing together new modular or platform technologies with novel approaches that include collaborative and virtual relationships to accelerate commercialization with shared capabilities and leveraged assets.  Vaccine development strategies combine knowledge and tools of biotechnology, microbiology, immunology, nanotechnology, and engineering with “out-of-the-box” thinking on formulation and packaging.  Cell culture-derived and attenuated vaccines (smallpox, influenza, biodefense, others), as well as human cell therapies, are being developed with new, high performance culture and cryopreservation medias that are free of animal components and other ingredients that add costs and steps to downstream processing, validation, and regulatory requirements.  Important to pandemic threats or surge needs, vaccines are being developed with features to accelerate commercialization, such as: (i) use of closed systems with disposable, cost-effective manufacturing steps; (ii) innovative formulation components, e.g., oral, nasal, patch; (iii) delivery without needles or other peripherals such as syringes; (iv) demonstrated potency, safety, and efficacy in protecting animal challenge models; (v) unit dose manufacturing; (vi) simple administration or self-administration options to eliminate needs for special training costs for vaccinators and minimal need for medical personnel; (vii) ease of distribution, preferably by reduction or elimination of the cold chain; (viii) reasonable price of product, including item cost to delivery, and storage and distribution costs; (ix) platform technologies to accelerate time-to-market; (x) multivalent or combination vaccines; and (xi) packaging to minimize medical waste and biohazards.  Regenerative medicine products are being similarly designed so that human cell and tissue therapies, as stand-alone or combination products, will provide effective therapeutic or intervention applications that are reasonably priced and meet regulatory requirements.  INCELL’s overall biotechnology product development approaches and strategies are to use novel solutions and modular platform technologies to make vaccines and regenerative medicine products that are effective, safe, and more easily administered and stored.  Integrated with that work, and INCELL’s contracts business, is the Texas BIO-Manufacturing Alliance Program (BIO-MAP) initiative which is intended to accelerate product commercialization through collaborative, synergistic partnerships among industry, academic, government, and research entities.

Dr. Mary Pat Moyer is a scientist, entrepreneur and technology business leader. She has founded or co-founded several companies, including INCELL and TEKSA. INCELL is a biotechnology company manufacturing products and providing contract services. TEKSA is an interdisciplinary technology business consortium-incubator with life sciences, engineering, and convergent technologies partners.  INCELL’s strong products pipeline focuses on vaccines, infectious disease, bio-manufacturing and regenerative medicine.  The pipeline includes non-needle vaccines (e.g., for smallpox, influenza, biodefense, others) and innovative platform technologies.  Moyer is a community activist and is passionate about growing Texas technologies.  In addition to many grants, publications, board positions and consultantships, her recognition includes numerous entrepreneur and leadership awards such as the E&Y Entrepreneur of the Year, AAUW “Breaking the Glass Ceiling”, and the 2006 Health Care Heroes Innovator award.  Her motto: “Be honest--hire wonderful people—keep a spiritual center and sense of humor—and never-never give up!”

Education: Florida Atlantic University-Biology, BS (1972); MS (1974); University of Texas at Austin-Microbiology, Ph.D. (1981).
