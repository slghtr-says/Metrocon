Advances in Intraocular Lens Technology
Mutlu Karakelle
Alcon Research, Ltd.

It has been a little over a half a century since the first intraocular lens (IOL) implantation by Harold Ridley on November 29, 1949, at St. Thomas Hospital in London.  Dr. Ridley replaced the cataractous natural crystalline lens of a patient with an artificial lens made of polymethyl methacrylate and initiated a significant change in the practice of ophthalmology.  Since then, millions of cataract surgeries have been performed and more than one hundred million intraocular lenses have been implanted with a remarkable success rate. Meanwhile, cataract surgery has been transformed from a major surgery with one week of hospitalization to an outpatient procedure with an actual surgery time of less than ten minutes for skilled surgeons. This remarkable improvement in the practice of cataract surgery has been achieved as a result of continuous improvements in surgical technique, instrumentation and intraocular lenses.

The first IOL material selected by Ridley was polymethyl methacrylate (PMMA). PMMA has a high light transmittance, relatively high refractive index, low specific gravity and desirable mechanical properties. It is biostable and chemically inert for long-term implantation in the intraocular environment. The advent of phacoemulsification and microsurgical techniques have enabled cataract surgeons remove a cataractous lens through a much smaller incision as compared to extracapsular cataract removal techniques.  This evolution created the need for a lens which could be folded and implanted through smaller incisions.  Initial foldable intraocular lenses were made, as were with many other new medical devices and implants, from readily available materials with appropriate properties and biomedical histories.  Polyhydroxyethyl methacrylate (polyHEMA) hydrogel and polydimethyl siloxane elastomer were the initial materials selected.  However, the desire for thin profile lenses required higher refractive index optic materials.  As a result, HEMA copolymer acrylic hydrogels and dimethyl siloxane-diphenyl siloxane copolymer silicone elastomers were introduced. Another approach was to design and synthesize a new intraocular lens biomaterial, based on unique requirements of this implant site and surgical procedure.  This approach has been remarkably successful in creating a satisfactory intraocular implant.

The advent of foldable intraocular lenses has completed the evolution of small-incision cataract surgery.  Availability of different foldable IOL materials has significantly contributed to the understanding of IOL biocompatibility.  IOL biocompatibility appears to be affected by many factors including material chemistry, purity, biostability, mechanical properties, surface chemistry and lens design.  An ideal biocompatible foldable IOL is well accepted by the surrounding ocular tissues, is biointegrated with the capsular bag, and provides satisfactory visual acuity during the patient’s lifetime without additional secondary surgical interventions. UV and blue light filtering capabilities of IOLs are important for the long-term health of the eye.  Today, cataract surgery is one of the most successful surgical procedures and greater than 95 percent of all cataract surgical procedures result in improved vision.  Intraocular lens is the most widely used and one of the most successful long-term implants and has made significant improvement in the quality of life of the cataract patients.

Mutlu Karakelle received his B.S. degree in Chemistry in 1978 and M.S. degree in Polymer Chemistry in 1979 from the Middle East Technical University, Ankara, Turkey.  He received his Ph.D. degree in Materials Science and Engineering in 1986 from the University of Utah, Salt Lake City.  He has been active in the biomedical device industry for over 20 years.  He joined Alcon Laboratories in 1992 and is currently Vice President of Surgical Products Research.  He published numerous articles and holds over 30 U.S. Patents.
